How I did the House Price Analysis

This page is under construction

To conduct the house price study, I started by downloading data from the Property Price Register. I tidied the data up in Excel, taking out any mistakes and adding a column for the Eircode and Townland. I knew I wanted to pull in the latitude and longitude, so I made some blank columns that I could fill in later and saved it as a .csv.

[picture of excel]

Pulling in the Lat/Longs was the first issue that I couldn't solve. I signed up for Google API and wrote some Python code that would hopefully read in the CSV, check the Eircode and append the coordinates into the two columns but for some reason it wouldn't return any coordinates. I tried again with the Open Street Map API, but that didn't work either.

[picture of python]

After trying a bunch of free APIs, I gave up and decided that I didn't need exact coordinates. I took the list of townlands, went on Google Maps and manually searched for each place. There wasn't too many so it didn't take too long. I then used a VLookup to pull them into the main sheet.

[picture of list of townlands]

Next, I wanted to create the graphic of the map. My workplace has an Arc GIS license so I started by pulling in the local authority/administrative areas shapefile, and the river and water bodies data, accessible from the OSI and the EPA through data.gov.ie. I fed in the .csv file and used the geoprocessing tool XY Table to Points to covert it into point data. As expected the points from each townland were overlapping. I searched for a jitter tool (I think it was Disperse Markers?? Can't remember) that allowed the points to be displayed at random distances from the original coordinates, without changing the data underneath.

[picture of arc pro]

At this point I decided to make things a little harder for myself. I wanted to use as much free and open source products as possible to make this process replicable by anyone. So I loaded all my GIS stuff into QGIS, a software I hadn't used in over 10 years (the horror). The equivalent jitter tool in QGIS unfortunately put everything into a perfect circle. No randomness - everything was organised too well.

[picture of qgis perfect circles]

So, I went back to Excel (though, if that's not available to you, Google Sheets, Open Office, Libre Office or Apple's 'Numbers' all function similarly with similar formulas). I used randbetween() to pick a distance from each lat and long. If doing this in Python you could use randint() from the random package. It took a few goes to figure out how far visually I wanted to move things, but now I had jittered the points.

[picture of randbetween()]

I loaded them back into QGIS and plotted them. I used the timeline feature to move between the years and exported them as a series of jpegs. I then put the jpegs into a slidedeck and exported them as a .gif.
